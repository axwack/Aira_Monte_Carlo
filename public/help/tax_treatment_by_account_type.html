Tax Treatment by Account Type

AiRA Reference · Federal rules · State treatment varies — check your domicile

How the IRS treats each dollar depending on where it lives. The account wrapper — not the underlying holding — determines the tax rate.

The four buckets

Taxable brokerage
After-tax contributions. Dividends and interest taxed each year. Gains taxed at sale. Most flexible — but most tax-exposed.
Pre-tax 401k / IRA
Pre-tax contributions. Grows tax-deferred. Every dollar withdrawn is ordinary income — no capital gains treatment, ever.
Roth IRA
After-tax contributions. Grows tax-free. Qualified withdrawals (59½ + 5-year rule) = 0% federal. No RMDs required.
HSA
Pre-tax contributions. Tax-free growth and withdrawal for qualified medical. After 65 non-medical = ordinary income, like a 401k.

Key concepts

The "taxable first" rule only applies if you have a taxable brokerage. If your portfolio is entirely pre-tax, Roth, and HSA, there are no long-term gains to harvest at preferential rates. Pre-tax withdrawals are always ordinary income regardless of what you held inside the account.
State domicile changes the math significantly. States that tax capital gains as ordinary income eliminate the federal 0% LTCG advantage. States with no income tax make taxable gains meaningfully cheaper. Your domicile before retirement is one of the highest-leverage tax decisions available.
The pre-tax trap. Large traditional IRA/401k balances generate forced ordinary income at RMD age, stacking with Social Security taxation and IRMAA surcharges. A Roth conversion ladder in the years before RMDs begin is the primary tool for reducing this exposure.
Optimal withdrawal order: Roth last (preserve tax-free compounding) · HSA for medical (0% always) · taxable next (LTCG rates) · pre-tax bracketed to avoid crossing into a higher tier. This order minimizes lifetime taxes across all states.
